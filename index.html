Letter of a young Intern :)

1. Intro

Hey there! If you are reading this you are probably beginning your VC experience at Shilling. Or elsewhere if this document actually becomes useful to onboard and educate young people who are joining this awesome ecosystem.

Basically the idea to start this document began as I understood I was accidentally storing a crazy amount of posts/articles or any kind of content on VC and startups, there is so much to read out there… And as I was doing this I thought that it could be interesting to aggregate and structure these materials. The main goal is to accelerate one's arrival to this job being a source of centralized knowledge for educational and research purposes.

The types of tools and content are very diverse and there are a lot of categories. But, I will separate them into two: VC content — how to invest, how does VC work, how to build conviction and much more. And the second category is startup content. I think it really is important to understand startups and how they work and get to know their components, constraints and limitations before starting to invest in them. On top of that it will be great to have a database from which you can share materials with the founders you are talking with, and try to add as much value as possible to them, creating a better relationship with them. In my opinion one of the most rewarding parts of the job.

Both categories were designed with this dual-use: education purpose for an upcoming VC and tools to help founders in their crazy journey.

4. Advice

There are many things I would suggest to someone starting their internship. First and foremost, one of the most important factors for growing within the team is context. Context has many components, but a key one is understanding past investments. I would make the effort to learn about every portfolio company: what they do, why we invested, and where they stand today. It's important to understand not only why some companies failed, but also which ones became the highlights of the portfolio. Context is crucial because, in many discussions, the team will reference these past investments or compare new opportunities to them. In terms of context, I'd also include getting to know the major startups, unicorns, and VC firms, their stories, business models, and highlights.

Adding to this, one lesson I learned later in the internship was the importance of having context on the deals that are currently moving forward. I found it extremely useful to stay up to speed on all active deals, join some of the calls when possible, and research the space. Doing so helps you add value to discussions and better understand the decision process. There's no comparison between joining a discussion knowing nothing and joining after spending 30/45 minutes researching, where you've already begun forming your own line of thought.

I don't think there's any other industry where staying up to date is more important than in VC. An investor needs to be on top of new trends and technologies launching every week: What new feature did OpenAI release this week, and how is it impacting the 1,000 agentic AI startups out there? But also, are there any major tailwinds creating opportunities in otherwise stable industries (e.g. defense in recent years)? And finally, how are the financial markets looking? How is liquidity across different private sectors? At what multiples are public tech companies trading, and why?

For product launches, I like to check the Product Hunt website and subscribe to their newsletter. For tailwinds and macro trends, I recommend listening to lots of podcasts: the Financial Times has a super useful daily 15-minute one, and one of my favorites is the 20VC podcast with Harry Stebbings. To finish, for numbers and financials, the Data-Driven VC newsletter is excellent — the intern email is subscribed, and I recommend reading every issue.

The fastest way to learn and grow as a VC is to talk to a lot of people: both founders and investors. If I were to do it all over again, I would try to book 20 short meetings (30 minutes each) every week. This makes your learning curve grow exponentially. Don't hesitate to cold-message anyone you find interesting.

Even taking just 5–10 minutes to prepare before each meeting makes a huge difference, not only for your own understanding but also for the other person, who will enjoy the conversation much more. And if you're speaking with a founder, showing that you've done your homework can even move you up their list of investors, as you come across as genuinely useful in the discussion.

I believe that if you see a huge number of deals, you'll eventually find something good. But the odds aren't great. A big part of this job is about increasing those odds, and that comes down to having better deal sources. This is where co-investors become really important. The conversion rates at each stage of the VC funnel are far higher when your deal flow comes through co-investors who can introduce you directly to founders, compared to just scanning startup lists from accelerators or relying on cold emails and LinkedIn inbounds.

To wrap it up on a broader note: I've heard this many times, and after this experience I really get it — being curious and challenging assumptions about how things have always been done is key. Don't be afraid to speak up and form opinions, there are no stupid questions. In many ways, the mindset of startup founders and VCs should be the same: question the status quo, look at problems from new angles, and push for better solutions instead of just accepting "this is how it's always been." For example you can stand out with things like: unicorns.pt made by Miguel Pinho from SeedCamp (Tier-1 VC in Europe).

Of course, doing all of this takes time. But honestly, it's a pretty easy job to like, because every day you're learning, meeting smart people, and constantly being forced to think differently. Make the most out of a job that enables possibilities that can't be found anywhere else. You'll be exposed to smart and hard-working people. Enjoy it!

Augusto Vasco Costa
